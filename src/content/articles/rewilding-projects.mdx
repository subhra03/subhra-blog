Rewilding is more than planting trees. It is an attempt to restore ecological processes that let landscapes regulate themselves. That can mean reconnecting habitats, reintroducing keystone species, or allowing rivers and forests to behave less like engineered assets.

Across Europe, rewilding projects are testing what recovery can look like at landscape scale.

## Species Can Rebuild Systems

Some animals change ecosystems by their behavior. Grazers shape vegetation. Predators influence movement patterns. Beavers alter waterways and create wetland habitat.

Reintroducing or protecting these species can trigger changes that manual restoration alone cannot easily reproduce.

## People Remain Part Of The Picture

Rewilding is not about removing people from land. Successful projects have to work with farmers, local communities, tourism, safety concerns, and cultural memory.

The strongest projects treat local support as part of the ecology, not an obstacle outside it.

## Measuring Success Is Complex

A restored landscape may look messy compared with managed land. Dead wood, shifting water, varied vegetation, and changing animal movement can all be signs of ecological function.

Success requires patience and better metrics than tidy appearance.

## Recovery At Scale

The promise of rewilding is that nature can do more repair work when humans stop interrupting every process. The challenge is creating enough space, trust, and continuity for that repair to happen.
