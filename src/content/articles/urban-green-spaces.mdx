Cities are dense by design, but density does not have to mean sensory overload. Parks, street trees, community gardens, and green rooftops can change how a city feels and how people recover from stress.

Green space is not decoration. It is part of public health infrastructure.

## Nature Lowers Cognitive Load

Urban life asks people to filter noise, traffic, crowds, and visual clutter. Natural environments provide a different kind of attention. They let the mind rest without becoming inactive.

Even brief exposure to trees, water, or planted areas can make a neighborhood feel less exhausting.

## Access Matters More Than Acreage

A large park is valuable, but small distributed spaces often matter more for daily life. A shaded bench, a planted median, or a pocket park near transit can serve people who do not have time to travel across the city.

Equity is central here. Green neighborhoods should not be available only to people who can afford higher rents.

## Design For Use, Not Just Beauty

The best urban green spaces invite ordinary routines. They have seating, shade, safe paths, lighting, and maintenance. They support walking, resting, playing, and gathering.

A green space that feels unsafe or inaccessible will not deliver the benefits that planners expect.

## A Healthier City Pattern

Cities can become calmer without becoming less vibrant. The goal is not to cover every street in plants. It is to make nature a regular, reachable part of urban experience.
